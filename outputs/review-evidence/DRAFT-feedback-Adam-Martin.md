## Q1: What are 1-2 specific things Adam does that have a positive impact on the team or outcomes?

Adam steps in to cover standup when Jon and I are both unavailable, which keeps the team's daily rhythm going without disruption. That reliability matters — the team doesn't lose momentum when there's no PO or PM in the room.

He also raises QA risk points during ceremonies, for example flagging gaps in test case coverage or highlighting where testing timelines are tight. That keeps quality concerns visible at the right moments and gives the team a chance to adjust priorities before problems land later in the cycle.

More broadly, Adam looks after his QA team — he acts as the first point of contact for his testers when they need guidance and manages their day-to-day workload. That people-management side of the role is important and he takes it seriously.

## Q2: What is one thing Adam could do differently to be more effective, and what would the impact be?

The biggest improvement Adam could make is giving the team more visibility into what QA is tracking day to day. At the moment, standup updates are consistently along the lines of "meetings all day" or "catching up with admin," which doesn't tell the team what QA is working on, what's blocked, or where things stand. That means we lack a clear picture of QA progress until something surfaces as an issue — and when it does come up in a ceremony without a prior heads-up, there's not enough context to respond constructively in the moment.

The Test Team Lead role is designed to be the coordination link between QA and the rest of the team. If Adam used standups to share what QA is actively testing, what's at risk, and where he needs input, it would transform how the team plans around quality. Even a short message ahead of planning or retro with the key QA risks would make a material difference to how quickly we can act on them.

The other area where Adam could step up is taking more visible ownership of core TTL deliverables — specifically the QA automation strategy and test documentation. At the moment, there isn't a clear view of automation priorities, AQA test card status, or pass/fail outcomes being shared with the wider team. Similarly, QA processes and best practices aren't being documented on Confluence in the way the role expects. Owning and communicating those outputs would give the team much more confidence in where QA stands and where it's heading.

## Q3: How does Adam contribute to (or hinder) team collaboration, communication, or decision-making?

Day to day, Adam and I don't interact much — communication between QA and the product side feels quite siloed. Most of our interaction only happens reactively, when there's already an issue or when he needs input on something specific. The TTL role is designed to bridge that gap, but at the moment the bridge isn't really active.

There was talk of me joining the desktop meetings where Adam, Josh, and Jon sync regularly, but those keep getting dropped as there's not enough desktop-specific content to sustain them. I think the right move would be to replace that with a regular QA-product sync — even fortnightly — focused specifically on what QA is seeing across the board: test progress, emerging risks, automation status, and anything that needs product input. That would give us a structured touchpoint to stay aligned, catch problems earlier, and make the coordination the role is designed to provide actually land.
